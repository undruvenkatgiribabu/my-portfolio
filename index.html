Leadership · Global Teams

From 80 to 200 Engineers: What I Learned Scaling a Global Delivery Team

Venkat Giri Babu Undru · May 2025 · 4 min read

When I took over the infrastructure portfolio, the delivery team had 80 engineers. By the time I handed it over, we had 200+. Running a $13M annual programme. With zero major escalations.

1. Structure before headcount

Before every ramp-up wave, we defined the operating model — the pods, the ownership boundaries, the escalation paths. When a new engineer joined, they knew exactly where they fit. Confusion is expensive at scale.

2. Invest in your Layer 2 leaders

Your senior managers and team leads are your real multipliers. I spent a disproportionate amount of time developing that layer — coaching them on client communication and decision-making under pressure. That investment compounded.

"Scaling a global team isn't a hiring exercise. It's an organisational design challenge. Most people get the order wrong."

3. Governance is not bureaucracy

At 80 people, you manage by presence. At 200, you manage by system. Weekly leads sync, bi-weekly delivery reviews, monthly executive QBRs — every meeting had a clear owner and tracked action items.

4. Culture doesn't scale automatically

We were explicit about our values: ownership, transparency, client-first. We recognised them publicly and called out drift when we saw it. The team knew what we stood for — and that held even across time zones.

Zero Escalations for 18 Months: How We Built a Culture of Delivery Accountability

Venkat Giri Babu Undru · June 2025 · 4 min read

Eighteen months. No escalations from the client. A managed services contract renewed without a competitive rebid. That result didn't happen by accident.

Make accountability a system, not a personality

Every issue had an owner, a resolution date, and a visible status. The system did the chasing so the managers didn't have to.

Kill the blame culture before it starts

We separated problem-reporting from performance evaluation. If you raised an issue early, that was a positive signal. If it got escalated to the client because you sat on it — that was a different conversation entirely.

"An escalation to the client means we already failed once internally. Our job is to solve it at the lowest possible level — every single time."

Run tight, predictable executive reviews

Our monthly QBRs followed a fixed format — SLA performance, incident trends, upcoming risks. No surprises. That trust is what got the contract renewed without a rebid.

Proactive risk management, not reactive firefighting

We maintained a live risk register reviewed weekly. Risks that were amber got a mitigation plan within 48 hours. Nothing sat.

Why Digital Infrastructure Transformation Is No Longer Optional — And What's Actually Blocking It

Venkat Giri Babu Undru · November 2023 · 5 min read

Every CIO I speak to knows that digital infrastructure transformation isn't a future priority. It's an overdue one. Yet most large organisations are still running on a patchwork of legacy systems held together by institutional knowledge and technical debt that compounds every quarter.

The cost of standing still is invisible — until it isn't

Legacy infrastructure doesn't send you a bill. It costs you in slower release cycles, brittle integrations, weekend incidents, and engineers spending 60% of their time keeping lights on instead of building value.

What's actually blocking transformation

  • 01Fear of disruption over fear of stagnation. Teams worry more about breaking something today than falling behind over the next three years.
  • 02No single owner of the transformation. When everyone owns it, nobody does.
  • 03Treating it as a technology project, not a business initiative. When business co-owns it, it becomes a competitive capability.
"The organisations that will win the next decade are not the ones with the best technology. They are the ones that built the delivery engine to absorb and operationalise that technology faster than anyone else."

Start with the outcomes, not the architecture. What does the business need to do in three years that it cannot do today? Work backward from that to the infrastructure capabilities required.

Five Modern Enterprise Problems That Cloud-Native Infrastructure Actually Solves

Venkat Giri Babu Undru · March 2024 · 5 min read

Cloud-native infrastructure, done properly, is the answer to the five most persistent operational problems enterprises face.

Problem 1: You can't get real-time visibility across your operations

Most large enterprises are still making decisions off data that's 24–48 hours stale. Cloud-native platforms built on event streaming (Kafka, Azure Event Hubs) enable genuine real-time operational intelligence.

Problem 2: Your systems can't scale when the business needs them to

Legacy infrastructure forces you to over-provision for the worst case — or fail when it arrives. Cloud-native architecture built on microservices and auto-scaling means your infrastructure scales with demand.

"Cloud isn't a cost play. Used correctly, it's the answer to the five biggest operational headaches enterprises face today — and the foundation for every competitive advantage they want to build tomorrow."

Problem 3: Incidents take too long to detect, diagnose, and resolve

Cloud-native observability compresses the resolution window dramatically. The goal is mean-time-to-resolution measured in minutes, not hours.

Problem 4: Releasing new features takes months, not days

Cloud-native paired with mature DevOps — CI/CD, containerisation, infrastructure as code — enables deployment frequencies unimaginable five years ago.

Problem 5: You can't attract or retain the engineering talent you need

The best engineers want to work on modern stacks. Cloud-native modernisation is not just a technology investment — it's a talent strategy.

ServiceNow · MSP Governance · SLA

ServiceNow as the Bible for MSP Performance: How to Govern Multi-Year Contracts Without Losing Strategic Partners

Venkat Giri Babu Undru · January 2024 · 7 min read

I have sat on both sides of the MSP governance table. The answer is building ServiceNow as your single source of truth for MSP governance — not as an afterthought, but as the operational backbone of the entire relationship from Day 1.

The core problem: penalty without intelligence is just punishment

Most enterprise-MSP contracts have well-written SLA clauses. The failure is not in the contract language — it is in the measurement infrastructure. When your only governance tool is a monthly SLA report reviewed in a room full of lawyers, you are managing consequences, not performance.

"The goal of MSP governance is not to catch vendors failing. It is to create a system where failure is detected early enough that it never becomes a crisis — for either party."

The two-track model: SIP vs. financial penalty

Track 1 — Yellow Zone (SIP): A SIP is triggered when a KPI slips below target for two consecutive months. The vendor gets a structured 30–90 day window with defined milestones to self-correct. No financial impact while SIP is active.

Track 2 — Red Zone (Penalty): Financial credits are reserved for P1 SLA breach, chronic breach of 3+ consecutive months, or failure to execute an active SIP. When a SIP fails, it converts automatically to contractual default.

Five ServiceNow modules that power the architecture

  • 01Contract Management module — SLA thresholds, penalty structures, SIP eligibility windows. Every governance action links back here creating an auditable chain of evidence.
  • 02Vendor Performance Management module — Monthly KPI records scored against contractual targets. Business rules automatically flag any KPI entering breached status.
  • 03SLA Management module — Real-time timers against every active P1/P2 incident. When restore window is breached, a service credit task is automatically generated.
  • 04SIP Records — Provisioned automatically by Flow Designer on two consecutive monthly breaches. Both enterprise team and vendor see the same record.
  • 05Financial Credit Workflow — Triggered by P1 SLA breach or SIP exit failure. Calculates credit and routes to procurement for invoice adjustment.
"When both parties are looking at the same dashboard in real time, governance becomes a conversation — not a confrontation."

The leadership dashboard: what each side sees

Enterprise leadership: RAG status across all active MSP contracts, active SIPs and milestone rates, financial credits accrued, P1 trends, contract renewal risk indicators.

Vendor delivery leadership: Live KPI performance vs. targets, open SIP milestones with due dates, active SLA timers on open incidents, rolling 12-month KPI trend, credit exposure if current trajectory holds.

ServiceNow, architected correctly, is not just a ticketing tool. It is the operating system for your vendor ecosystem. Build it that way from Day 1.

Move to Production: Why the Hardest Part of Any Transformation Programme Is the Last 30 Days

Venkat Giri Babu Undru · February 2024 · 6 min read

In my experience running large-scale cloud and infrastructure transformation programmes, the move to production — what we call MTP — is where more value is destroyed than at any other point in the programme lifecycle. Not because the technology fails. Because the handover fails.

"Operations doesn't fail at MTP because they lack capability. They fail because nobody involved them early enough for capability to matter."

Fix 1 — Governance that connects delivery to operations from day one

A standing weekly governance call at both leadership and leads level with four fixed agenda items: inflight and pipeline projects; sustain lead alignment (a named person per project, not a team); milestone tracking; and issues and blockers surfaced and owned in this forum — not in an email chain.

Fix 2 — Nearing completion milestone

When a project crosses 70–80% completion, three deliverables are mandatory: entry/exit criteria, a co-authored MTP checklist with evidence, and a runbook written by delivery and validated by operations.

The MTP checklist — six buckets, every item evidenced

A checklist without evidence is a list of intentions. Every item requires a screenshot, test result, signed email, or configuration export.

📋 Bucket 1 — Programme & reporting
  • MTP report generated before and after migration
  • Business criticality of the application formally documented
  • Recovery priority (Tier 1/2/3) confirmed and aligned with DR runbook
  • Go-live sign-off email and risk acceptance email — Archer exception details attached
🖥️ Bucket 2 — Infrastructure & platform readiness
  • Supported version and latest patch applied — exceptions require formal risk acceptance
  • Switchover evidence and app connectivity test on standby — tested and evidenced, not assumed
  • Availability of lower environments (DEV, SIT, UAT) confirmed post-migration
  • Cronjob details — schedule, owner, dependency, and failure procedure documented
🔐 Bucket 3 — Security & compliance
  • Wallet configuration confirmed, tested, and accessible. Connection strings verified
  • Guardium installation confirmed active and reporting. Security team sign-off attached
  • VA score post migration — open critical/high findings require remediation plan before go-live
  • Privileged account details (SYS, SYSDG, wallet) rotated, vaulted, and access tested by ops
  • DB related passwords (sys, sysdg, wallet etc.) handed over, vault entry verified, old credentials invalidated
💾 Bucket 4 — Backup, recovery & HA/DR
  • Backup configuration confirmed — first successful backup post-migration verified restorable
  • Rubrik SLA details confirmed — policy active, tier matching recovery priority, screenshot attached
📡 Bucket 5 — Monitoring & observability
  • OEM monitoring active — target added, metrics collecting, alert thresholds configured, location field updated
  • Monitoring disabled on source (older) system — stale alerts mask real incidents on new environment
🎫 Bucket 6 — ITSM & operational handover
  • DBR/SNOW CMDB entries updated — host details, app mapping, support group, recovery tier
  • DBA assigned in DBR — a named individual, not a team queue, accountable for Day 1 support
  • OEM location updated as FIELD — incorrect location breaks auto-routing of alerts to the right team

Fix 3 — Cutover window: shadow and reverse shadow

Shadow (1–2 weeks): Operations watches delivery execute procedures — monitoring checks, alert responses, escalation paths. Observes and takes notes.

Reverse shadow (1–2 weeks): Operations executes. Delivery watches and corrects in real time. Runbook gaps surface and get fixed before go-live — not after.

Common issues you will face

  • ▸Not every ops resource knows about the project. Mandate a formal ops team briefing at the nearing completion milestone — not just the lead, the whole team.
  • ▸More volume than expected. Build 20–30% buffer into the MTP calendar. Capacity surprises at cutover are programme killers.
  • ▸Escalation procedure breaks down during the handover window. Define a specific handover window escalation path and publish it to both teams before the window opens.
"A production transition is not an event. It is a process that starts at programme kick-off and ends 30 days after go-live. Treat it that way, and your operations team will thank you. Treat it as a hand-off meeting, and your operations team will be calling you at 2am for the next six months."
